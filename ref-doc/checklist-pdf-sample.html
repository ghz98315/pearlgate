Before you pay a deposit

Run these 7 checks.

After 12 years on the manufacturing side, I've learned that the buyers who get burned almost always skipped the same handful of checks. Here's the short version of what I run on every supplier.

1

Business Legitimacy

Real factory, or a trading company in disguise? Verify the business license and that the registered scope of business actually includes manufacturing — not just wholesale/trade.

Red flag: company name on the quote doesn't match the certificates or the bank account.

2

Certification — Required, or Nice to Have?

First ask whether your market legally requires a formal cert. If yes, expect a higher price + minimum volume (a real cert carries recurring cost, amortized over units). If not, a factory that truly builds to standard may not hold a live cert — what matters is a genuine certificate or a full test report (IEC 61851-1 / UL 2594 / IEC 62752).

Red flag: a forged or "borrowed" certificate — or neither a cert nor any test data.

3

Production Capacity

Can they deliver your volume, on your timeline? Ask how much capacity is already committed, and for their last 3 months' real on-time delivery rate — not a promise.

Red flag: vague answers on current capacity utilization.

4

Quality Control System

Do they test every unit, or sample and pray? For safety-critical tests like hipot and ground continuity, the answer must be 100%, not sampled. Look for a working aging station and calibrated equipment.

Red flag: hipot test logs that look identical across batches — a sign of fabricated data.

5

Component & BOM Transparency

What's actually inside? The #1 cost-down trick is the golden-sample bait-and-switch. Under-spec copper in a charging cable runs hot — a real fire risk.

Red flag: refusal to disclose component brands, or cable weight noticeably below spec.

6

Target-Market Compliance

Right standard, right connector, right paperwork. CCS1 and CCS2 are physically incompatible — ship the wrong one and the whole container is scrap.

Red flag: the supplier never asks which market you're shipping to.

7

Communication & Responsiveness

Pre-sale is a supplier's best behavior. If responses are slow, vague, or non-technical now, it gets far worse once your money is gone and there's a dispute.

Red flag: only a salesperson relaying messages, never an engineer.

⚠

If you only have time for three, never skip #2, #4, and #6. Those are the ones that turn into total losses — not just headaches.
