# 第 1 帖 — 别逼供应商都给 UL/CE(CTA)

> 反直觉立人设。普通中介喊"一律要认证",你说"先搞清需不需要"。

```
"Is it UL certified?"

It's the first question most buyers ask a Chinese EVSE supplier. And it's often the wrong one.

Here's what 12 years on the manufacturing side taught me:

A formal certificate — a UL Listing, a CE Notified-Body assessment — carries real recurring cost. Annual fees. Factory audits. Re-testing. That cost only makes sense when it's amortized over volume.

So a lot of legitimate factories build to the full test standard — hipot, insulation, temperature rise, the works — but don't hold a live certificate. Not because the product fails. Because the paperwork isn't worth it for their typical order size.

No certificate ≠ a bad product.

The right questions are two separate ones:

1. Does YOUR market actually require it?
A US retail channel or AHJ may demand a UL/ETL mark. An EU importer is liable for CE conformity. If so — you need the real, current certificate, and you should expect a higher price and a higher minimum order to absorb the cert cost.

2. Does the factory actually meet the standard — with or without the cert?
If no certificate is required, ask for the full test report instead: tested against IEC 61851-1 / UL 2594 / IEC 62752, by whom, on what date. A factory that genuinely builds to standard can show the data.

The real red flag isn't "no certificate."

It's a forged or borrowed certificate — or neither a cert NOR any test data, with vague answers when you ask for the report.

Buyers who understand this stop overpaying for paperwork they don't need, and stop trusting paperwork that proves nothing.

If you're sourcing chargers or cables from China and want a second pair of eyes on whether you actually need certification — and whether a supplier truly meets it — my DMs are open.

```

# 第 2 帖 — 100% hipot vs 抽检(纯价值)

```
"We test our chargers" sounds reassuring.

Then you ask one follow-up question and the whole thing falls apart:

"Do you hipot-test every unit, or a sample?"

Here's why that question matters.

A hipot (dielectric withstand) test checks whether the insulation can handle high voltage without breaking down. On EV charging equipment — which sits between the grid and a human — this isn't optional. A unit that fails hipot in the field is a shock or fire risk.

The cost-cutting shortcut: test a few units per batch instead of every unit. It's invisible. The samples pass. The paperwork looks clean. And the one defective unit in the batch ships anyway.

In 12 years of manufacturing QC, this was one of the most common corners I saw cut — and one of the easiest to hide.

What a serious factory does:
→ 100% hipot on every single unit (not a sample)
→ Calibrated equipment with a current calibration sticker
→ Test logs that vary unit-to-unit (identical logs across a batch = a red flag for fabricated data)

When you tour a factory, don't just ask "do you test?"

Ask "show me the hipot station running, and show me yesterday's logs."

The answer to that tells you more than any certificate.
```

# 第 3 帖 — 充电线缆偷铜(纯价值)

```
There's a fire risk hiding in cheap EV charging cables, and you can catch it with a kitchen scale.

The trick is called under-spec copper.

A charging cable is quoted with a certain conductor cross-section — say 6mm². That copper is what carries the current. It's also the single most expensive material in the cable.

So it's the first thing a cost-cutting supplier shaves.

You get a cable labeled 6mm² that's actually running 4.5mm² inside. It looks identical. It plugs in fine. The sample you approved was the real thing.

Then mass production quietly switches to thinner copper.

Under-spec copper runs hotter than it should under load. On a cable carrying 32A for hours, "hotter than it should" is not a small problem.

How to sanity-check without a lab:
→ Copper is heavy. Weigh the cable. A cable with the right copper has a predictable weight — significantly under it is a flag.
→ Ask for the conductor cross-section in writing, on the spec sheet AND the invoice.
→ For anything serious, cut a sample and measure the actual conductor (this is standard at incoming QC for a reason).

The golden-sample-then-swap is the oldest trick in sourcing. Copper is where it hits you hardest in EVSE.
```

# 第 4 帖 — CCS1/CCS2 整柜报废(评论区带博客链接)

```
The most expensive sourcing mistake in EV charging isn't a quality defect.

It's shipping the right product with the wrong connector.

CCS1 and CCS2 look almost identical. Same communication protocol. Same power levels. They are physically incompatible.

CCS1 → North America, Korea
CCS2 → Europe, UK, Australia, most of the world

Ship a container of CCS1 cables to a European buyer and you don't have a quality problem. You have scrap. The product can be flawless and it's still a total loss.

What makes this dangerous:
→ It's 100% avoidable, so nobody double-checks it
→ A supplier focused on the US market may default to CCS1 without asking your destination
→ "It works everywhere" is a sentence that should end the conversation

The fix is boring and it works:
Confirm the connector standard, in writing, against the destination market — before the deposit, not after the container ships.

I wrote a full breakdown of CCS1 vs CCS2 (pins, markets, why two standards exist) — link in the comments.

# 第 5 帖 — 贸易公司 vs 工厂(CTA)

```
You found a great "factory" on a B2B platform. Good price, fast replies, photos of a production line.

There's a decent chance it's not a factory at all.

A large share of "manufacturers" on sourcing platforms are trading companies. They take your order, subcontract to a real factory you never see, and mark it up.

Nothing illegal about that. But it changes everything:
→ You lose price control (there's a middleman margin you can't see)
→ You lose quality control (you can't audit a factory you don't know exists)
→ You lose traceability when something goes wrong

How to tell the difference:
→ Read the business license scope (经营范围). "Manufacturing" terms vs. only "wholesale/retail."
→ Check registered capital against claimed production scale — a tiny number behind a big factory is a flag.
→ Does the company name on the quote match the name on the certificates and the bank account? Mismatches are common with traders.

None of this means "never work with a trader." Sometimes that's the right call.

It means: know which one you're dealing with, and price accordingly.

After 12 years in Chinese manufacturing, telling factories from traders is almost reflex for me. If you've got a supplier you're unsure about, I run a 7-point verification on a specific factory — license, certs, capacity, QC, the lot. Happy to talk through it.

# 第 6 帖 — 认证成本经济学(CTA)

```
A factory quoted you a charger with no UL certificate. Most buyers walk away.

That might be a mistake — and here's the math behind it.

A UL Listing isn't a one-time stamp. It's an ongoing cost:
→ Initial testing and engineering review
→ Annual maintenance fees
→ Quarterly factory follow-up inspections (you pay for the inspector's time)
→ Re-testing when anything in the BOM changes

For a factory whose typical order is a few hundred units, that cost per unit is brutal. So they build to the UL standard — same components, same construction — but skip the certificate itself.

Selling into US big-box retail or need AHJ sign-off? You need the real, current certificate — and you should expect to pay more and order more to absorb that cost.

Selling B2B, or into a market that doesn't enforce it? A factory that meets the standard without the paperwork may be exactly right — verify with the full test report instead.

The expensive mistake isn't "buying from a factory without UL."

It's paying the certified premium when your market never required it — or skipping verification entirely and assuming "no cert" means "unsafe."

12 years in manufacturing taught me to ask "do you need it?" before "do they have it?"

# 第 7 帖 — golden sample 调包(纯价值)

```
The sample was perfect. The 5,000-unit shipment was not. This is the oldest trick in sourcing, and it has a name.

It's called the golden sample.

A factory makes one flawless unit by hand — premium components, careful assembly — and sends it for your approval. You approve. You pay. Then mass production quietly switches to cheaper parts.

In EV charging this isn't just a quality annoyance. It's a safety gap:
→ A relay rated for fewer cycles than the sample's
→ A thinner internal wire gauge
→ An RCD module from an unbranded supplier
→ Enclosure plastic that's no longer the flame-retardant grade you signed off on

By the time a unit fails in the field, the container's been sold.

How to defend against it:
→ Keep your approved golden sample sealed — it's your reference for any dispute
→ Require a pre-shipment inspection that opens random mass-production units and compares them to it
→ Get the BOM in writing and make "no substitutions without written approval" a line in the PO
→ For safety-critical parts, ask for the component brand on the box, not just "equivalent"

A golden sample proves a factory CAN build it right. It doesn't prove they WILL at volume.

That gap is where buyers lose money.
```

# 第 8 帖 — IP65 真假(纯价值)

```
A wallbox spec sheet says "IP65." That single number is one of the most over-claimed specs in EV charging.

IP65 means dust-tight and protected against water jets — the baseline for an outdoor wallbox. The problem: anyone can print "IP65" on a datasheet. Far fewer can prove it.

What a real IP65 claim requires:
→ An actual ingress protection test report (dust chamber + water jet), not just a number
→ Sealed cable glands and gaskets that match — a great enclosure with a cheap gland is not IP65
→ The rating applied to the assembled unit, not just the empty housing

Why it matters for EVSE specifically: a wallbox lives outdoors for years, through rain, dust, and temperature swings. A unit that's "IP65 on paper" but leaks at the cable entry will corrode, trip, or fail — often just after the warranty conversation gets difficult.

How to check:
→ Ask for the IP test report and the test house
→ Look at the gland and gasket quality in person or on video
→ Ask whether the rating covers the full assembled product

"IP65" is a claim. The test report is the proof. Always ask for the proof.

# 第 9 帖 — MOQ 谈判(纯价值)

```
"Our MOQ is 1,000 units." Most buyers hear a wall. I hear a starting point — and so should you.

A factory's minimum order quantity isn't arbitrary. It usually comes down to three things:
→ Tooling/setup cost they need to amortize
→ Minimum component purchase from THEIR suppliers
→ Whether your order is worth interrupting a production run for

Once you understand that, MOQ becomes negotiable in ways most buyers never try:
→ Accept the factory's standard color/spec instead of full customization — kills the tooling argument
→ Combine your order with their existing production run of a similar model
→ Order a smaller first batch at a slightly higher unit price, with a committed follow-on
→ Ask what THEIR component MOQ is — sometimes the "1,000" is really "we must buy 1,000 connectors," which you can work around

The buyers who get stuck are the ones who treat MOQ as a fixed rule.

The ones who get deals understand what's driving it, and solve that instead.

This is true across sourcing, but it's especially useful in EVSE where customization (connector type, cable length, plug standard) is often what's inflating the MOQ in the first place.
```

# 第 10 帖 — 连接器才是关键部件(纯价值)

```
In an EV charging cable, the part most likely to fail isn't the cable. It's the connector.

People obsess over copper gauge and ignore the two ends — where most field failures actually happen.

Why connectors fail:
→ Contact resistance: a poorly made pin heats up under load. Heat degrades the contact. Higher resistance, more heat — a runaway loop that ends in a melted connector.
→ Mating cycles: a Type 2 connector is rated for thousands of insertions. A cheap one loses spring tension early and starts arcing.
→ Strain relief: the bend point where cable meets connector is where flexing cracks the seal. Skimp here and water gets in.
→ Temperature sensors: quality connectors have a thermal cutoff. Cheap ones don't — so when contact resistance climbs, nothing stops it.

When I evaluate a charging cable supplier, I spend more time on the connector than the cable itself:
→ Contact resistance test data (not just a spec)
→ Mating cycle test results against IEC 62196
→ A physical look at the strain relief and gland
→ Whether there's a real temperature sensor inside, or just a claim

A cable is simple. A connector is where the engineering — and the cost-cutting — actually lives.

#EVCharging #EVSE #ChargingCable #Connectors #Sourcing
```

**备注**:最硬核,展示垂直深度(IEC 62196、接触电阻、温度传感器)。无 CTA,专业度本身即信任。
