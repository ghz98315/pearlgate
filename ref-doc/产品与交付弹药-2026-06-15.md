## The 7-Point EVSE Supplier Verification Checklist
*For buyers sourcing portable chargers, charging cables, and wallboxes from China*

After 12 years inside BYD manufacturing — moving from NPI Engineer to Engineering Manager to Quality Manager, running quality for global brands like Dell, Toshiba, Lenovo, Huawei, Siemens, and ASUS — I've learned that most sourcing failures aren't bad luck. They're predictable, and they're catchable before you wire a single dollar.

Here are the 7 checks I run on every supplier before recommending them. Most buyers skip 5 of these.

### 1. Business Legitimacy — Is this a real factory, or a trading company in disguise?

**What to check:**
- Business license (营业执照) — verify the registered company name, registration number, and registered capital
- Cross-check the registration number against China's national enterprise credit system (国家企业信用信息公示系统)
- Confirm "scope of business" (经营范围) actually includes manufacturing, not just trade/wholesale

**Why it matters:** A huge share of "factories" on B2B platforms are trading companies that subcontract to real factories you never see. You lose price control, quality control, and traceability. Nothing inherently wrong with a trader — but you must *know* which one you're dealing with.

**Red flags:**
- Registered capital suspiciously low for claimed production scale
- Business scope lists only "wholesale/retail" with no manufacturing terms
- Company name on the quote doesn't match the name on certificates or the bank account

---

### 2. Certification — Do you actually need it, and does the factory truly meet the standard?

This is the check most buyers get backwards. They demand "UL certified" or "CE certified" across the board — and either overpay for paperwork they don't need, or get fooled by a certificate that proves nothing.

The smarter framing is two separate questions: **(A) does your market legally require formal certification, and (B) does the factory actually meet the test requirements — with or without the certificate?**

**Reality most buyers don't know:** Plenty of legitimate factories build to the full test standard (hipot, insulation, temperature rise, etc.) but **don't hold a live certificate**. A UL Listing or a CE Notified-Body certificate carries real recurring cost — annual fees, factory audits, re-testing — that only makes sense when amortized over volume. For a small buyer in a market that doesn't enforce it, insisting on a certified product just raises your unit price and forces a higher MOQ. **No certificate does not mean the product fails the standard.**

**So check, in order:**
1. **Does your target market / sales channel legally require it?** (US retailer/AHJ may require UL/ETL; EU importer is liable for CE conformity; marketplaces have their own rules.) If yes → you need the real, current certificate, and expect a higher price + minimum volume to amortize the cert cost.
2. **If a certificate is presented — is it genuine and current?** CE: self-declaration vs. Notified Body (4-digit NB number). UL/cUL/ETL: verify the file number in the issuer's public database. Match the certificate's model to what you're buying; check the expiry date.
3. **If there's no certificate — can they prove the product still meets the standard?** Ask for the full test report against IEC 61851-1 / IEC 62752 / UL 2594, the test house, and the date.

**Real red flags (the dangerous ones):**
- A forged or "borrowed" certificate (belongs to another company / model)
- Neither a certificate nor any test data — vague answers when you ask for the report
- Certificate expired, or model "close but not identical" to your order
- Supplier insists you need a costly certification when your market clearly doesn't — or hides that the certified price requires a much higher MOQ

**Standards to map against:** EVSE general safety IEC 61851-1 · connectors IEC 62196 · comms ISO 15118 · US wallbox/EVSE UL 2594, connectors UL 2251 · portable IC-CPD IEC 62752

---

### 3. Production Capacity — Can they deliver your volume, on your timeline?

**What to check:**
- Number of production lines for your specific product type (injection / cable extrusion / PCB SMT / assembly)
- Monthly capacity vs. your order volume — and how much is *already committed*
- Lead time honesty: ask for their last 3 months' actual on-time delivery rate, not their promise

**Why it matters:** A factory quoting a great price but running at 95% capacity will push your order to the back. "60-day lead time" becomes 110 days, and your container misses the season.

**Red flags:** vague answers on capacity utilization · claimed capacity inconsistent with visible floor space/line count · no willingness to show a video walk-through

### 4. Quality Control System — Do they test every unit, or sample and pray?

**What to check:**
- **Hipot (dielectric withstand)**: 100% (every unit) or sampled? Ask their test voltage and duration, and check it against the level the product's own safety standard requires. A supplier who can't state their hipot parameters off the top of their head usually isn't running it as claimed.
- Ground continuity / bonding on every unit
- For cables: conductor resistance, insulation resistance, pull/flex testing
- Aging / burn-in station — present and actually used?
- Final functional test before packing

**Why it matters:** The difference between a factory and a workshop. EVSE handles high current near humans — a hipot failure that ships is a fire/shock risk. Sampling instead of 100% is the most common shortcut, invisible until a unit fails in the field.

**Red flags:** "we test samples" for safety-critical tests · hipot logs identical across batches (fabricated data) · no aging station / powered off during visit · test equipment with expired/missing calibration sticker

**Your edge:** 12 years on exactly this side. Fabricated test data has tells — you know where to look.

---

### 5. Component & BOM Transparency — What's actually inside?

**What to check:**
- Brand/grade of safety-critical components: relays, RCD/RCBO, MCUs, connectors
- Cable: actual copper cross-section vs. claimed (under-spec copper is rampant)
- Enclosure: flame-retardant grade (e.g. UL94 V-0), certified or just claimed
- Is the quoted BOM the mass-production BOM? (the golden-sample swap)

**Why it matters:** The #1 cost-down trick is the bait-and-switch: perfect golden sample, then cheaper parts at volume. Under-spec copper runs hot — a real fire risk.

**Red flags:** refusal to disclose component brands · cable weight below spec (copper is heavy) · resists pre-shipment inspection of mass production

---

### 6. Target-Market Compliance — Right standard, right connector, right paperwork?

**What to check:**
- Connector matches destination: Type 1/J1772 (NA), Type 2/Mennekes (EU), GB/T (CN), NACS (NA growing)
- Cables: CCS1 vs CCS2 are physically incompatible — wrong one = scrap
- Voltage/frequency: 120/240V 60Hz (NA) vs 230/400V 50Hz (EU)
- Portable plug: NEMA (NA) / Schuko-CEE (EU) / BS1363 (UK)
- Market paperwork: CE+UKCA, FCC, etc.

**Why it matters:** The most expensive mistake because it's 100% avoidable and 100% fatal — a perfect product with the wrong standard is a total loss.

**Red flags:** supplier doesn't ask which market you ship to · "it works everywhere" · no experience exporting to your region

---

### 7. Communication & Responsiveness — The cheapest predictor of the relationship

**What to check:** response time/quality during quoting · technical questions answered technically or deflected · English-capable engineer or only sales · willing to do a live video walk-through

**Why it matters:** Pre-sale is best behavior. Slow/vague/non-technical now = far worse after a dispute when your money's gone.

**Red flags:** days of silence during quoting · technical questions bounced to "our engineer will confirm" with no follow-up · only a salesperson, never an engineer

---

### How to use this checklist
Run all 7 before you wire a deposit. If stretched, never skip **#2 (certification), #4 (QC), #6 (market compliance)** — those turn into total losses, not just headaches.
